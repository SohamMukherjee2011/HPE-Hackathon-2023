What is Cyber Security?

Cyber security refers to the practice of protecting computer systems, networks, and data from unauthorized access, theft, damage, or other types of cyber attacks. Cyber attacks can take many forms, including phishing, malware, ransomware, denial-of-service attacks, and hacking.

Cybersecurity refers to the protection of digital systems, networks, devices, and data from unauthorized access, theft, damage, or disruption. It involves the use of various technological and procedural measures to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information and resources in the digital world. Cybersecurity is essential in today's interconnected world, where cyber threats such as hacking, malware, phishing, and ransomware are becoming increasingly common and sophisticated. Effective cybersecurity practices include network and system monitoring, regular updates and patches, strong authentication, data encryption, access control, and incident response planning.

Major cyber security threats

  • Ransomware: This type of malware encrypts the victim's files and demands payment, usually in the form of cryptocurrency, in exchange for the decryption key. Recent high-profile ransomware attacks include WannaCry, Petya/NotPetya, and the Colonial Pipeline attack.
  • Phishing: This is a type of social engineering attack in which an attacker uses a fraudulent email, text message, or website to trick a user into providing sensitive information or clicking on a malicious link. Phishing attacks have been used in many high-profile breaches, including the 2016 Democratic National Committee email leak.
  • Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs): These are targeted attacks that are often carried out by state-sponsored or highly organized cybercriminal groups. APTs use sophisticated tactics to gain access to a victim's network and can remain undetected for long periods of time. The APT group known as APT29, or Cozy Bear, has been linked to several high-profile attacks, including the SolarWinds hack.
  • Insider Threats: These are threats that come from within an organization, such as employees or contractors who intentionally or unintentionally cause harm to the organization's data or systems. Insider threats can be difficult to detect and prevent, and can result in significant damage. The 2013 breach at Target, which resulted in the theft of millions of customer credit card numbers, was caused by an insider who had access to the company's payment systems.
  • Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) Attacks: These attacks involve overwhelming a website or network with traffic, causing it to become unavailable. DDoS attacks have been used to take down major websites, including Twitter, GitHub, and Amazon Web Services.

    Ways to neutralise cyber security threats

    Cyber security has evolved significantly since the early days of computing. Initially, cyber security was focused on protecting physical assets such as mainframe computers and servers. As technology evolved, so did the threats and vulnerabilities. With the rise of the internet, cyber security became more focused on protecting networks and devices from unauthorized access and data breaches. As the use of cloud computing and mobile devices increased, cyber security evolved to include protection for these platforms. Today, cyber security has become an essential aspect of all digital systems, and its importance continues to grow as cyber threats become more sophisticated and complex. Advancements in technology have enabled the development of new and more advanced cyber security tools and techniques, including machine learning and artificial intelligence, to help detect and prevent cyber-attacks..

  • Network Security: This involves securing networks and devices that are connected to the internet or other networks. Network security measures include firewalls, intrusion detection and prevention systems, and virtual private networks (VPNs).
  • Application Security: This involves securing applications against cyber attacks such as SQL injection, cross-site scripting (XSS), and other types of vulnerabilities that may be exploited by hackers. Application security measures include secure coding practices, vulnerability assessments, and penetration testing.
  • Cloud Security: This involves securing data and applications that are hosted in the cloud. Cloud security measures include data encryption, access controls, and security monitoring to ensure that data is protected against unauthorized access.
  • Endpoint Security: This involves securing endpoints such as laptops, smartphones, and other devices that are connected to a network. Endpoint security measures include antivirus software, encryption, and access controls to ensure that only authorized users can access sensitive data.
  • Data Security: This involves securing data at rest and in transit. Data security measures include encryption, access controls, and data loss prevention (DLP) technologies to prevent unauthorized access and ensure that data is not lost or stolen.

    • Conclusion
